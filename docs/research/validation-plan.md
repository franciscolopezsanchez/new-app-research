# Guarderías España — Discovery Phase

**Version**: 1.0
**Date**: March 2026
**Status**: Active — Pre-build validation
**Owner**: Founder

## Overview

This document is the working guide for validating the core hypothesis before writing a single line of code. It covers the methodology, the phone script, the signal codebook, and the decision framework.

The goal is not to sell anything. The goal is to find out if a real, painful, recurring problem exists — and whether directors would pay to solve it.

---

## Section 1. Validation Goals

### 1.1 Hypotheses Being Tested

| # | Hypothesis | What "Validated" Looks Like | What "Invalidated" Looks Like |
|---|---|---|---|
| H1 | Directors feel real pain with WhatsApp-based communication | Director spontaneously describes a specific incident that caused them stress, conflict, or lost time | Director says "WhatsApp works fine, we've had no issues" — and can't recall a single difficult moment |
| H2 | Teachers suffer from after-hours messages on personal phones | Director or teacher has complained about this; director has tried to address it; it's mentioned without being prompted | Director doesn't recognize the problem; says teachers are fine with it |
| H3 | Parents are anxious about not knowing what their child is doing | Director reports parents frequently asking for updates, calling to check in, or expressing worry | Director says parents trust the school and rarely ask for updates |
| H4 | GDPR/LOPDGDD non-compliance is a real concern | Director mentions GDPR unprompted, has received a complaint or warning, or expresses discomfort with photos in WhatsApp groups | Director says "we just send photos on WhatsApp, no one has complained" with zero anxiety |
| H5 | Directors would pay ~€59/month for a solution | Director currently pays for operational SaaS tools (accounting, classroom management) and sees communication tools as a category worth spending on | Director says all tools are free; school has no software budget; decision is made by a central office |
| H6 | The problem is worth solving NOW | Director has actively looked for alternatives, researched apps, or tried something other than WhatsApp | Director has no urgency: "it's fine as is, maybe someday" |

### 1.2 The Validation Signal — What "Confirmed" Means

A single call is considered a **positive validation signal** when at least ONE of the following occurs without being prompted:

- The director tells a specific story about a WhatsApp incident that caused real consequences (a parent complaint, a conflict between teachers, a data breach scare, a misunderstanding that escalated)
- The director says they have thought about finding an alternative or have already tried one
- The director expresses anxiety about GDPR or photo-sharing without being asked about it directly
- The director mentions teacher complaints about personal phone use
- The director references having a budget for school software or asks about pricing unprompted

### 1.3 Validation Threshold

**Minimum to proceed to build:**
- 15–20 completed discovery conversations
- At least 10 of those conversations produce at least one strong positive signal (defined above)
- At least 5 directors mention GDPR or data privacy without being directly asked
- At least 5 directors describe a specific painful incident — not a general complaint, a real story

**Do not proceed if:**
- Fewer than 7 of 20 calls produce any positive signal
- The most common response to "¿qué problemas tienes con la comunicación actual?" is "ninguno en particular"
- No director has tried to find an alternative to WhatsApp

### 1.4 Sample Size Rationale

Fifteen to twenty conversations is sufficient for discovery-phase qualitative research when targeting a narrow, specific segment (Spanish guardería directors). The goal is not statistical significance — it is pattern saturation. At 10–12 conversations, if no strong patterns are emerging, that is itself a signal. If by conversation 8 every director is telling the same story, that is strong confirmation.

## Section 2. The Mom Test — Applied to This Context

Rob Fitzpatrick's core principle: ask about their life, not your idea. People will lie to be polite when you talk about your product. They cannot lie about what has already happened to them.

### 2.1 Questions We Must NEVER Ask

These questions will produce false positives. A polite director will always say "yes" to them:

- "¿Usarías una app que reemplazara el WhatsApp?" — invites validation of the concept, not the problem
- "¿Crees que los profesores necesitarían una herramienta mejor?" — leading and hypothetical
- "¿Te parecería útil tener todo centralizado en un solo sitio?" — everyone says yes to abstract convenience
- "¿Estarías dispuesto a pagar por algo así?" — asked directly and early, this is meaningless
- "¿Te preocupa el GDPR?" — if you mention GDPR first, any "yes" is a mirror response, not a real concern
- "¿Crees que los padres querrían saber más sobre el día a día de sus hijos?" — of course they would. This tells you nothing.

### 2.2 Questions That Reveal Truth

These work because they ask about past behavior and real events:

- "¿Cuál es la herramienta que más usáis para comunicaros con las familias?" — neutral, descriptive, reveals current behavior
- "Cuéntame la última vez que hubo un malentendido o un problema con una familia relacionado con la comunicación" — requires a real story, not an opinion
- "¿Alguna vez has buscado alguna herramienta alternativa al WhatsApp? ¿Qué encontraste?" — reveals whether there's enough pain to motivate action
- "¿Qué parte de la comunicación con las familias te lleva más tiempo cada semana?" — specific, time-bound, behavioral
- "¿Cómo gestionáis el tema de las fotos? ¿Habéis tenido alguna vez alguna familia que pusiera algún reparo?" — indirect GDPR probe through concrete behavior

### 2.3 How to Avoid Pitching While Still Getting Signal

The single biggest risk in these calls is that the founder accidentally pitches. This happens in subtle ways:

- Describing the problem as a "big challenge in the sector" — you're framing before they've felt it
- Reacting enthusiastically to pain signals: "Yes! That's exactly what we're working on!" — shuts down honesty
- Filling silence with product descriptions when they pause
- Mentioning that you're "building something" early in the conversation

The discipline: act like an anthropologist, not a salesperson. You are there to document their world, not to change it. Say less. Listen more. When they describe pain, ask them to say more — don't validate it with excitement.

## Section 3. Phone Call Script

### Pre-Call Preparation

Before each call, note:
- School name, location, size (if known), type (privada / concertada)
- How you found the contact (LinkedIn, Páginas Amarillas, referral, cold)
- Any context from their website (their age range, apparent size, any tech mentions)
- Your specific goal for this call: get through all 10 discovery questions OR identify 2–3 strong signal moments

---

### PART A — El Opener (30 segundos)

**Scenario 1: Answers directly (the director)**

> "Hola, buenos días. ¿Es [Nombre del director/a]?
>
> Perfecto. Me llamo [Tu nombre], y te llamo porque estoy haciendo una investigación sobre cómo gestionan la comunicación con las familias las guarderías en España — nada de ventas, te lo prometo. Solo quiero entender cómo funciona el día a día en centros como el vuestro.
>
> ¿Tienes cinco minutos ahora, o te viene mejor que te llame en otro momento?"

**Por qué funciona:** No menciona producto, no hace pitch, pide permiso explícito, es breve.

---

**Scenario 2: Answers a receptionist or secretary**

> "Hola, buenos días. Me llamo [Tu nombre] y quisiera hablar con la directora o el director del centro, si es posible. No es ninguna venta — estoy haciendo una pequeña investigación sobre la gestión de centros educativos y me gustaría hacerle unas preguntas rápidas. ¿Está disponible un momento?"

If the gatekeeper asks for more detail:

> "Claro, estoy investigando cómo los centros de educación infantil gestionan la comunicación con las familias — si usan alguna herramienta, qué funciona mejor, ese tipo de cosas. Nada comercial, es solo para entender el sector. ¿Crees que podría hablar con ella/él un par de minutos?"

If they say the director is not available:

> "Sin problema. ¿Cuándo sería el mejor momento para llamar? ¿Mañana a primera hora quizás?"

---

### PART B — Calificacion (1 minuto)

Once you have the director on the line:

> "Estupendo. Para confirmar que eres la persona adecuada con quien hablar: ¿tú eres quien se ocupa de la comunicación con las familias en el centro, o hay alguien más involucrado en eso?"

If they share the role with someone:

> "Perfecto, genial. Para el tipo de preguntas que tengo, tú serías la persona indicada de todas formas — me interesa más entender cómo funciona el día a día que los detalles técnicos."

Confirm scope:

> "¿Cuántos niños tenéis aproximadamente en el centro? [pause] ¿Y de qué edades?"

This gives you context on size and complexity without being intrusive.

---

### PART C — Preguntas de Descubrimiento (10–15 minutos)

**Transition into the questions:**

> "Genial. Pues si te parece, te hago unas preguntas sobre cómo gestionáis la comunicación — sin ningún orden en particular, y si en algún momento te parece que algo no es relevante para vosotros, me lo dices y pasamos a otra cosa. ¿Te parece?"

---

**Pregunta 1 — Estado actual (comportamiento, no opinión)**

> "Para empezar: ¿qué herramientas usáis ahora mismo para comunicaros con las familias? No solo la principal — todas las que usáis."

Listen for: WhatsApp groups, paper notes, email, an app, verbal communication. Note which they mention first — that is the dominant tool. Note if they sound proud or apologetic when they describe their current setup.

---

**Pregunta 2 — Frecuencia y volumen**

> "¿Con qué frecuencia os comunicáis con las familias? ¿Cuántos mensajes diría que se mandan al día, aproximadamente?"

Listen for: overwhelm signals, mentions of multiple groups, high volume. The word "muchos" without context is weak; "a veces no doy abasto" is strong.

---

**Pregunta 3 — Incidente reciente (la pregunta más importante)**

> "Cuéntame la última vez que hubo algún malentendido o algún problema con una familia relacionado con la comunicación. Puede ser algo pequeño — no tiene que ser grave."

This is the most important question. Do not rush it. If they pause, wait. If they say "no recuerdo ninguno," probe gently:

> "A veces pasa que un mensaje llega tarde, o que una familia no ve la información a tiempo... ¿os ha pasado algo así?"

Listen for: a real story with a real consequence. A name (anonymized). An emotion. "Tuvimos que llamar por teléfono," "la madre se puso muy tensa," "fue un momento incómodo." If they still have nothing, that is a data point — note it.

---

**Pregunta 4 — Carga de trabajo de los profesores**

> "¿Cómo gestionan los profesores la comunicación con las familias — lo hacen desde el móvil personal, desde el centro, hay algún protocolo?"

Do NOT say "¿Los profesores se quejan de recibir mensajes fuera del horario?" — that is leading.

Listen for: "desde el móvil personal" is a signal worth noting. "A veces les llegan mensajes por la noche" even more so. If they volunteer "es un tema que hemos tenido que hablar con el equipo," that is a strong signal.

If they mention personal phones, follow up:

> "¿Y eso ha sido alguna vez un tema de conversación en el equipo?"

---

**Pregunta 5 — Fotos y contenido multimedia**

> "¿Cómo gestionáis el tema de compartir fotos con las familias? ¿Tenéis algún proceso para eso?"

This is an indirect GDPR probe. Do NOT mention GDPR here.

Listen for: "las mandamos por WhatsApp," "tenemos un grupo," "cada profe manda desde su móvil." Follow up:

> "¿Alguna vez alguna familia ha puesto algún reparo en cómo se comparten las fotos, o en que sus hijos salieran en fotos con otros niños?"

If they show any hesitation or mention privacy:

> "¿Eso es algo que os preocupa o que hayáis tenido que gestionar formalmente?"

---

**Pregunta 6 — GDPR (solo si no ha surgido organicamente)**

Only ask this if GDPR has NOT come up naturally. If it has, skip or use as a follow-up.

> "¿Tenéis algún proceso para gestionar los consentimientos de las familias — para fotos, para comunicaciones, ese tipo de cosas?"

Do NOT explain GDPR to them. Do NOT say "la normativa obliga a..." Just ask about their process and observe how they respond. A director who says "sí, tenemos formularios de consentimiento que firmamos al inicio del curso" and sounds relaxed is different from one who says "bueno, sí, supongo que deberíamos tenerlo más formalizado" with discomfort.

---

**Pregunta 7 — Tiempo invertido**

> "Si tuvieras que estimar cuánto tiempo dedicas tú personalmente a la comunicación con familias en una semana normal — hablamos de responder mensajes, informar de incidencias, coordinar con los profesores sobre qué comunicar — ¿cuánto sería?"

This surfaces the time cost without you framing it as a problem. Let them quantify it themselves.

---

**Pregunta 8 — Expectativas de los padres**

> "¿Cómo describirías las expectativas de las familias en cuanto a la comunicación? ¿Han cambiado en los últimos años?"

Listen for: "cada vez piden más," "quieren saber todo lo que pasa," "a veces es agotador." These indicate demand-side pressure. Contrast with: "las familias aquí son muy tranquilas, confían en el centro" — less urgency.

---

**Pregunta 9 — Alternativas buscadas**

> "¿Alguna vez has buscado alguna herramienta o app específica para gestionar la comunicación con familias? ¿Qué encontraste?"

This is the behavior signal for H6 (urgency). If they have searched, the problem is real enough to motivate action. If they have tried something, even if it failed, the pain is real.

Listen for: "sí, miré ClassDojo," "probamos una app pero los padres no la instalaron," "busqué pero no encontré nada que me convenciera." These are all strong signals.

If they say no:

> "¿Y no habéis sentido la necesidad de buscar algo diferente a lo que tenéis ahora?"

---

**Pregunta 10 — Presupuesto para herramientas (indirect willingness-to-pay)**

> "¿Usáis algún software de gestión en el centro — para administración, contabilidad, gestión de matrículas, ese tipo de cosas?"

If yes:

> "¿Y eso lo pagáis vosotros o va por la administración? ¿Tienes idea de cuánto os cuesta aproximadamente?"

This reveals whether they have a software budget and a habit of paying for tools. It is NOT a willingness-to-pay question for your product — it is a behavioral question about existing habits.

---

**Pregunta 11 — Solución ideal (open, no leading)**

> "Si pudieras cambiar una sola cosa de cómo gestionáis ahora la comunicación con las familias, ¿qué sería?"

This is a free-response question. The answer tells you what THEY prioritize, not what you think they need. If their answer aligns with your solution, good. If their answer is something you hadn't considered, even better.

---

**Pregunta 12 — Cierre de descubrimiento**

> "Para terminar: ¿hay algo que sientas que no te he preguntado, o algún aspecto de la comunicación con familias que creas que es importante y que no hayamos tocado?"

This occasionally produces the most honest and useful response of the entire call.

---

### PART D — Cierre (2 minutos)

> "Esto es todo lo que necesitaba. De verdad, muchísimas gracias — esto ha sido muy útil. Me has dado mucho en qué pensar.
>
> Estamos en una fase muy temprana de investigación, y en algún momento, cuando tengamos algo concreto que enseñar, me gustaría volverte a contactar para que le eches un vistazo y me des tu opinión. ¿Te parecería bien?
>
> [If yes]: Perfecto. ¿Sería mejor un email o una llamada breve?
>
> Por último — y no hay ninguna obligación — ¿conoces a algún otro director de guardería que creas que podría tener este mismo tipo de desafíos? Me ayudaría mucho hablar con más personas del sector."

Note any referrals immediately — these are warm leads and tend to produce higher-quality conversations.

---

### PART E — Manejo de Objeciones

**Objecion 1: "No tengo tiempo ahora"**

> "Por supuesto, lo entiendo perfectamente. ¿Cuándo sería un buen momento para llamarte — mañana a primera hora o algún otro día esta semana? No te robo más de diez minutos."

If they seem genuinely rushed, don't push:

> "Sin problema. Te mando un email breve con mi contacto y si en algún momento tienes un hueco y te apetece charlar, me escribes. ¿A qué dirección te lo mando?"

---

**Objecion 2: "Ya usamos WhatsApp y funciona bien"**

Do NOT challenge this directly. Agree and get curious:

> "Me alegra escuchar eso — hay muchos centros que están muy contentos con ello. Solo por curiosidad: ¿ha habido alguna vez algún momento en que WhatsApp se os haya quedado corto, aunque sea algo pequeño? Me ayuda mucho entender los casos en que funciona bien y en qué situaciones no."

If they still insist everything is perfect:

> "Perfecto. ¿Puedo preguntarte cómo gestionáis el tema de los consentimientos para fotos — tenéis algún proceso formalizado para eso?"

This is your GDPR pivot. If they brush it off too: this school may not be your customer. Note it and move on.

---

**Objecion 3: "¿Me estás intentando vender algo?"**

Be completely direct:

> "No, para nada. Todavía no tengo nada que vender. Estoy en una fase de investigación pura — necesito entender si el problema que creo que existe realmente existe antes de construir nada. Si al final construyo algo y no te interesa, ningún problema. Lo que me interesa ahora mismo es escuchar, no vender."

The honesty here is your best tool. Directors respect directness.

---

**Objecion 4: "No somos los que tomamos estas decisiones" (cadenas / franquicias)**

> "Entendido. ¿Y en vuestra organización, quién sería la persona que toma este tipo de decisiones sobre herramientas para los centros? ¿Habría alguien con quien pudiera hablar?"

If they can connect you to the central decision-maker, follow that lead. If not:

> "De todas formas, tu perspectiva como directora del día a día es muy valiosa para mí — aunque no seas quien decide la herramienta, eres quien la usa. ¿Te parecería bien seguir con unas preguntas sobre tu experiencia actual?"

Many times, the operational director has significant influence even in chains. And their lived experience is valid research data regardless.

## Section 4. Signal Codebook — What to Listen For

### Strong Positive Signals (Mark as GREEN)

Use these to score calls and identify the strongest prospects for follow-up.

1. **The Incident Story**: Director tells a specific story — with details, emotions, or consequences — about a communication failure. "Una madre me llamó enfadada porque no había visto el mensaje de la excursión." The specificity is the signal. Generic complaints are not.

2. **The GDPR Spontaneous Mention**: Director mentions data protection, consent, LOPDGDD, or discomfort with WhatsApp photos without being asked. "La verdad es que el tema de las fotos me tiene un poco preocupada últimamente."

3. **The Active Search**: Director has already looked for alternatives. "Sí, miré ClassDojo pero los padres no lo instalaban." "Probamos una app hace dos años pero no funcionó." The attempt itself proves the pain was real enough to act on.

4. **Teacher Complaints Volunteered**: Director mentions teacher burnout or personal phone boundaries without being asked. "Mis profesoras se quejan de que les llegan mensajes a las diez de la noche."

5. **Time Quantification That Surprises Them**: Director estimates their communication time and visibly realizes it is more than expected. "A ver... pues igual dos horas al día, contando todo. Eso es mucho, ¿verdad?"

6. **Budget Awareness + Urgency Combination**: Director mentions paying for other software AND expresses that communication is a current headache. "Sí, pagamos por [X software], y la verdad es que la comunicación con familias es lo que más caos genera."

---

### Weak or Neutral Signals (Mark as YELLOW)

Interesting, worth noting, but not sufficient on their own.

1. **General Dissatisfaction Without a Story**: "WhatsApp no es lo ideal, la verdad." Noted, but they can't or won't tell you why. Could be mild or could be unexplored real pain.

2. **GDPR Awareness After Being Asked**: They show knowledge of GDPR requirements but only when you ask. Shows they are not totally naive, but not enough pain to volunteer it.

3. **Curious About What You Are Building**: They ask what you are building before the end of the call. Interest is not the same as pain, but it indicates they see the space as real.

4. **Mentions Problems Only at Other Schools**: "Yo no he tenido problemas, pero sé de otros centros que sí." Could be deflection, could be genuine. Worth asking for the referral.

---

### Negative Signals (Mark as RED)

These suggest this school is not a priority target or the hypothesis does not hold for them.

1. **Zero Recall of Any Communication Problem**: Director cannot name a single difficult moment, misunderstanding, or inconvenience, even with gentle probing. Communication is simply not on their radar as a problem space.

2. **No Agency Over Tool Decisions**: School is part of a large chain where all decisions are centralized. The director has no influence and cannot connect you to who does.

3. **Parents Described as Completely Passive**: "Las familias confían en nosotros, no nos preguntan mucho." Low parent engagement means low parental anxiety, which removes one of the core demand drivers.

4. **No Software Budget and No Interest in Having One**: "Nosotros usamos todo gratuito — no tenemos presupuesto para estas cosas." Combined with no urgency, this school will not become a paying customer.

### Call Log Template

Fill in within 30 minutes of the call, while memory is fresh. Use a spreadsheet, Notion table, or equivalent.

| Field | What to Record |
|---|---|
| Date | Date of call |
| School Name | Name of the center |
| City | Location |
| Type | Privada / Concertada-Privada / Pública |
| Size | Approx. number of children enrolled |

| How Found | Cold / LinkedIn / Referral (from whom) |
| Call Duration | Minutes |

| Software Budget Exists? | Yes / No / Unknown |
| Agreed to Follow-Up Demo? | Yes / No |

| Top Quote | One verbatim quote that captures the call |
| Score | 1–5 (see scoring guide below) |
| Notes | Anything unusual, surprising, or worth flagging |

---

### Call Scoring Guide (1–5 Scale)

**Score 5 — Strong Validation**
Director told at least one specific incident story AND showed one additional strong signal (GDPR, teacher burnout, active search for alternatives). Would clearly benefit from a solution and seems like they would pay for one. Agreed to follow-up.

**Score 4 — Clear Signal**
Director showed 2+ strong positive signals. May not have a dramatic incident story, but the problem is clearly real for them and they engaged thoughtfully with the questions. Agreed to follow-up or gave a referral.

**Score 3 — Moderate Signal**
Director showed 1 strong positive signal and the overall conversation felt like there is a real underlying problem. Some hesitation or lack of urgency, but the pain exists. Worth following up.

**Score 2 — Weak Signal**
Director was polite and engaged but produced only yellow signals. Problem may exist at a low level of intensity. No urgency. Would not be an early adopter.

**Score 1 — No Signal / Wrong Fit**
Director showed no interest in the problem space, cannot make tool decisions, or is clearly not in the target segment.

---

### Follow-Up Rules

- **Score 5 or 4**: Follow up within one week via email. Reference something specific they said. Offer to show a prototype or mockup "when it's ready — probably in a few weeks." Keep them warm.
- **Score 3**: Follow up in 3–4 weeks. Shorter email. Re-qualify before investing in the relationship.
- **Score 1 or 2**: No follow-up unless they specifically asked for it. Move on.

**Follow-up email template (Score 4–5):**

> Asunto: Re: Nuestra conversación del [fecha]
>
> Hola [Nombre],
>
> Muchas gracias por tu tiempo el otro día. Me quedé pensando en lo que me comentaste sobre [referencia específica a algo real que dijeron — el incidente, la preocupación, el tiempo invertido].
>
> Cuando tengamos algo concreto que enseñar, me encantaría que le echaras un vistazo y me dijeras qué te parece. Serías de las primeras personas en verlo.
>
> Te escribo cuando esté listo. Un saludo,
> [Nombre]

## Section 6. Validation Decision Framework

### Intermediate Checkpoint — After 8 Calls

Review after call 8. Ask:

**Question 1**: Have at least 4 of the 8 calls produced a Score 3 or above?
- If yes: continue with the current script and target profile. The signal is there.
- If no: something is wrong. Either the script is extracting the wrong things, or the target segment is off.

**Question 2**: Is there a consistent pattern in what people are saying — the same problems coming up independently, without prompting?
- If yes: you are approaching saturation. The signal is probably real.
- If no (every call is different, no common threads): the problem may not be universal enough. Consider whether you are targeting the right segment.

**Question 3**: Has anyone mentioned GDPR or data privacy unprompted?
- If none of the first 8 have mentioned it: either the sample is wrong (too small, too rural, too old-school) or H4 is weak. Do not abandon — adjust sample to include more urban, more recently opened centers.

**Adjustments to consider at the 8-call checkpoint:**
- If calls are short and directors are disengaged: tighten the opener and the value framing of the research. You may not be earning the conversation quickly enough.
- If everyone says WhatsApp is fine: try probing GDPR earlier, or ask about a recent inspection or audit.
- If the sample is skewing large (centers with 100+ children): adjust targeting to smaller private centers of 20–50 children, who have less institutional support and more personal ownership of the problem.

---

### Final Decision Framework — After 20 Calls

**Proceed to build if ALL of the following are true:**

1. At least 10 of 20 calls scored 3 or above
2. At least 5 directors mentioned GDPR or data privacy without being prompted
3. At least 5 directors told a specific incident story (not a general complaint — a real event)
4. At least 3 directors said they have searched for or tried an alternative to WhatsApp
5. At least 8 directors agreed to a follow-up demo

**Proceed with caution (validate one more segment) if:**
- You hit the signal thresholds but ALL strong signals came from directors in major cities (Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia) — test 5 more calls in secondary cities before committing
- All strong signals came from recently opened centers — may indicate a generational split. Older centers may resist change. Understand your real addressable market.

**Do not proceed (pivot required) if:**
- Fewer than 7 of 20 calls scored 3+
- Fewer than 3 directors mentioned GDPR unprompted
- No director has ever searched for an alternative to WhatsApp
- The most common response to the "one thing you'd change" question is something unrelated to communication (staffing, rent, administration)

---

### Pivot Options If Validation Fails

| Scenario | What It Suggests | Pivot Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Directors say no problem, but teachers mention it in passing | The director is not the right entry point | Explore teacher-led or bottom-up adoption. Change ICP. |
| Strong signal on GDPR but weak signal on pain | Fear, not urgency. Compliance buyer, not pain buyer | Reframe as compliance tool. Change messaging and pricing model. |
| Strong signal on teacher burnout but directors don't see it as their problem | Disconnect between user and buyer | Test whether teachers have any purchasing influence or whether there's a parent association angle |
| Willingness to pay is there but only for very small amounts (under €20/month) | Price sensitivity or perceived low value | Revisit pricing model. Consider per-classroom pricing or freemium with paid tiers. |
| No validation in Spain but strong interest from Latin American contacts | Wrong geography | Consider whether the market is better in LATAM first. Very different regulatory context. |

## Appendix A — Call Preparation Checklist

Before each call:

- [ ] Know the school name, size, type, and location
- [ ] Have the script in front of you (but do not read from it — know it)
- [ ] Have a blank call log template ready to fill in during/after
- [ ] Set a timer for 15 minutes so you know when to start wrapping up
- [ ] Have a follow-up email template ready to send within the hour
- [ ] Turn off notifications on your phone so you are fully present
- [ ] If recording: have consent language ready and ask at the start ("¿Te importa si grabo la llamada solo para mis notas? No lo comparto con nadie.")

Where to find guardería directors in Spain:

1. **Páginas Amarillas / Google Maps**: Search "guardería" + city. Filters by type and size. High volume, cold outreach.
2. **LinkedIn**: "Directora guardería" or "Directora escuela infantil" — better for warm outreach, lower volume.
3. **AMEI-WAECE** (Asociación Mundial de Educadores Infantiles): Professional association with directory.
4. **Fedepe** and regional equivalents: Federation of private education centers.
5. **Facebook groups**: "Directoras de guarderías España" — some private groups exist. Use to post a research request, not to cold message.
6. **Referrals from completed calls**: Every call ends with a referral request. These warm leads convert at significantly higher rates.

**Priority profile for first 10 calls:**
- Private center (not public — no budget authority)
- 20–80 children enrolled (small enough that the director feels the problem personally)
- Urban or peri-urban location
- Opened in the last 10 years (higher likelihood of tech awareness and GDPR sensitivity)
- Director is also a co-owner or founding member (highest decision-making authority)
