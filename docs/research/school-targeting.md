# School Targeting Strategy — Validation Call Phase

**Product:** School-parent communication app for Spanish preschools (ages 0–6)
**Phase:** Problem validation (pre-build)
**Goal:** 20–30 completed discovery conversations with school directors
**Last updated:** March 2026

---

## 1. Targeting Strategy Overview

### How Many Schools to Target in Total

**Recommended outreach list size: 150 schools**

The math:

| Stage | Rate | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Schools on list | — | 150 |
| Connect rate (director picks up or responds) | 25% | ~38 connected |
| Completes full 10–15 min conversation | 70% of connected | ~26 completed |
| Unusable (wrong person, chain school slipped through, etc.) | ~10% discard | ~23–24 usable |

A 25% cold connect rate is realistic for Spanish guarderías. The director is often also the manager, receptionist, and head teacher — they answer phones unpredictably. Some will be in class, pick up and hang up, or defer to a secretary who takes a message that goes nowhere. Building a list of 150 gives you a comfortable buffer to reach 25+ high-quality conversations without burning through your list in the first week.

**Do not target exactly 50 schools and expect 25 conversations. You will run out of list at the worst possible time — mid-insight, before patterns stabilize.**

Why 150 specifically (not 100, not 200):
- At 100, one bad week (holiday, flu season, school trip week) leaves you short
- At 200, the research overhead of scoring and populating the table becomes a part-time job for a solo founder
- 150 is a one-person manageable list that can be scored in a weekend using EDUCABASE exports

### Geographic Strategy

**Recommended: Targeted regional concentration, not a random national spread**

The case for concentration: If you eventually run a pilot, proximity reduces logistics cost dramatically. Schools in the same city can be visited in one day for in-person demos. Word of mouth within a city's private school director network is fast — a director who had a good call will mention you to a peer at the next AMPA meeting or sector event. This is a network-effect advantage you lose with a geographically scattered list.

The case for some diversity: Regional behavior does vary. Madrid schools face intense competition and may be more innovation-receptive. Basque Country schools are well-funded and may have existing solutions. Andalucia schools may have more acute pain due to fewer resources. Getting 3–4 regions represented gives you enough signal to know whether the problem is universal or geographically specific.

**On the Catalan language question:** Schools in Catalunya — especially those operating under the linguistic immersion model — may prefer communication in Catalan. For a 10-minute validation call, Spanish (castellano) is universally understood and professionally acceptable. The director will not hang up because you called in Spanish. However, if you send a pre-call email in Spanish to a school with a clearly Catalan name and Catalan-language website, expect a slightly lower open rate. A practical workaround: write the email template in Spanish but acknowledge it ("Sé que muchos centros en Catalunya trabajáis en catalán — si preferís continuar en catalán, sin problema"). This signals respect without requiring you to run a separate Catalan outreach track. For the validation phase, do not over-engineer the language dimension.

**Where private guarderías are densest in Spain:**

The private and concertado-privado sector for ages 0–6 is heavily concentrated in large urban areas. The regions with the highest density of private guarderías (ages 0–3, ciclo 1) and private escuelas infantiles (0–6, ciclos 1+2) are:

1. **Comunidad de Madrid** — Largest concentration by absolute number. The 0–3 cycle is almost entirely private in Madrid because public provision is severely underfunded at that age. High income levels, high tech-awareness among directors, strong willingness to pay for tools.
2. **Catalunya** — Second largest market, especially Barcelona and its metropolitan area (L'Hospitalet, Badalona, Sabadell, Terrassa). Dense urban private school ecosystem.
3. **Comunitat Valenciana** — Valencia city plus Alicante have significant private sector. Growing middle class, high mobile penetration.
4. **Andalucía** — Large by population but lower private school density. Sevilla and Málaga have a viable private sector. Málaga specifically has strong growth due to digital economy immigration.
5. **País Vasco** — Small in absolute numbers but high quality and high willingness to pay. Bilbao and San Sebastián schools are well-funded. High operational pain due to complex regulatory environment.
6. **Galicia / Castilla y León** — Lower density but independent operators dominate (few chains), which fits your ideal profile.

**Recommended geographic mix for the first 150 schools:**

| Autonomous Community | Schools | % of List | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Comunidad de Madrid | 50 | 33% | Largest private market, easiest to pivot to in-person pilot, highest tech-awareness |
| Catalunya | 30 | 20% | Strong private sector, Barcelona pilot potential, tests language dimension |
| Comunitat Valenciana | 25 | 17% | Growing market, underserved by existing tools, good size-fit schools |
| Andalucía | 20 | 13% | Sevilla + Málaga focus; tests whether problem is acute in less-resourced schools |
| País Vasco | 15 | 10% | High LTV potential, tests premium willingness to pay |
| Galicia or Castilla y León | 10 | 7% | Tests non-urban, high-independence operator profile |

This is a **Madrid-first strategy with regional diversification**, not an equal-weight national sample. That is intentional. Madrid will be your first pilot city if validation succeeds, so you want maximum density of warm contacts there.

### Timing Strategy

**Best days to call:**

- Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday are the strongest days
- Monday mornings are chaotic (week setup, parent drop-off issues, staff briefings)
- Friday afternoons: directors mentally checked out, leaving early, handling end-of-week admin

**Best time windows:**

| Window | Quality | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 09:30–11:00 | Excellent | After morning drop-off rush (08:00–09:15) has settled. Director is at desk. |
| 12:00–13:00 | Good | Pre-lunch window. Avoid 13:15 onward — lunch starts and schools get chaotic. |
| 16:00–17:00 | Acceptable | Post-afternoon-snack, before afternoon pickup at 17:00–18:00. Risky. |

**Do NOT call during:**

- 08:00–09:30: Drop-off. The director is at the door greeting families. You will get a rushed hang-up or the receptionist.
- 13:00–16:00: Lunch and nap time. This is sacred in Spanish preschools. Staff are on staggered breaks, directors are eating or handling nap supervision issues. Response quality drops significantly.
- 17:00–18:30: Afternoon pickup. Same as morning drop-off — operationally the most intense 90 minutes of the day.
- Any day during a school festival week (Navidad, Carnaval, Semana Santa, Fin de Curso)

**Calendar events to avoid (Spain, academic year 2025–2026):**

| Period | Dates (approximate) | Reason to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Semana Santa | Late March / early April 2026 | Schools closed or on half-schedule; directors traveling |
| Puente de mayo | 1–4 May 2026 | Reduced availability; school trips common |
| Fin de curso prep | Last 2 weeks of June | Directors consumed with end-of-year activities, portfolios, parent meetings |
| Summer (julio–agosto) | July–August | Most guarderías run reduced July service and close in August |
| September re-apertura | First 2 weeks of September | Enrollment chaos, new staff onboarding, parent anxiety peak |
| Navidad | 20 Dec – 8 Jan | Closed or minimal staff |

**Best months to run validation calls:**

Ranked best to worst:
1. **October–November**: Schools are settled after September chaos. Directors have a clear picture of the year. No major holidays. High receptiveness to new ideas.
2. **February**: Post-January, pre-Carnaval. Stable period. Good call quality.
3. **March (before Semana Santa)**: Solid, but watch the Semana Santa cutoff carefully — it varies by region.
4. **April (post Semana Santa)**: Strong, especially second half of April.
5. **May**: Good, but avoid the last two weeks as end-of-year prep starts.

Given you are starting in late March 2026: Begin immediately this week with Madrid and Valencia contacts. Pause outreach the week before Semana Santa (approximately March 23–April 5, 2026 depending on region). Resume with force in April — you will have the entire April–May window which is high quality.

## 2. School Scoring Criteria

**Total score: 0–10 points**
**Call priority threshold: 7+ = call first, 5–6 = call second wave, 4 or below = deprioritize**

Each of the 6 scoring factors below is worth 0, 1, or 2 points. The maximum is 10 (two factors are worth up to 1 point each, four are worth up to 2 points each — see details below).

---

### Factor 1: School Type (Titularidad)
**Weight: 2 points**

**Why it matters:** Public schools (centros públicos) cannot buy your product — purchasing decisions require municipal or regional government approval, budget cycles of 12–18 months, and procurement processes. Concertado schools have a split profile: concertado-privado schools behave like private schools (director has budget authority), while concertado-público schools behave more like public schools. Pure private schools are your ideal customer — the director controls the budget and can sign up same day.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 2 | Privado (fully private, no public funding) |
| 1 | Concertado-privado (private operator receiving partial public subsidy, director has budget autonomy) |
| 0 | Público or concertado-público (public funding dominates, purchasing decisions are not the director's to make) |

**How to find this data:** EDUCABASE exports include the field "Titularidad/Financiación" or similar. In the Ministerio de Educación school search (https://www.educacion.gob.es/centros/buscarCentros.do), each school listing shows "Titularidad: Privada / Pública / Concertada" and "Financiación" separately. Export the full dataset as CSV and filter on these two fields.

---

### Factor 2: Age Range Served (Ciclos de Educación Infantil)
**Weight: 2 points**

**Why it matters:** A school that serves only Segundo Ciclo (ages 3–6, which is state-funded and essentially free in Spain) has a radically different commercial profile than one serving Primer Ciclo (ages 0–3). Primer Ciclo is fee-paying, parents are highly anxious about the development of very young children, and communication is most intense. Schools serving both cycles (0–6) have the maximum communication burden and the maximum pain. Schools serving only ages 3–6 often operate more like a mini-primary school, with less intense daily parent communication needs.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 2 | Serves Primer Ciclo (0–3) — either alone (guardería) or combined 0–6 |
| 1 | Serves 0–6 but Primer Ciclo enrollment is very small (under 15 children) |
| 0 | Serves only Segundo Ciclo (3–6) |

**How to find this data:** EDUCABASE and the Ministerio search include "Enseñanzas impartidas" (educational stages offered). Filter for "Educación Infantil Primer Ciclo" or "Educación Infantil (Ciclos 1 y 2)." Schools listed only under "Educación Infantil Segundo Ciclo" score 0. Note: many school records show both cycles listed even if actual Primer Ciclo enrollment is tiny — this needs a quick sanity check on the school's website (look for "0–3" or "bebés" on their homepage).

---

### Factor 3: School Size
**Weight: 2 points**

**Why it matters:** A school with fewer than 15 children falls under your free tier — they will never pay, and their problems are less acute (the director knows every family personally and communicates informally). A school with 80+ children is likely a chain or a large cooperative with a committee-based purchasing process. The sweet spot is 15–80 children: small enough that the director is hands-on and feels the pain personally, large enough to justify a SaaS subscription and have complexity in parent communication.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 2 | 20–80 children enrolled (estimated) |
| 1 | 15–20 children (borderline payable) or 80–120 (larger, possible chain) |
| 0 | Under 15 children (free tier only) or over 120 (chain/large operator) |

**How to find this data:** EDUCABASE includes number of "unidades" (classrooms/groups) but not always direct enrollment counts. A school with 3–8 unidades in Educación Infantil typically has 15–80 children. More than 8 unidades suggests a large school. Cross-reference with Google Maps: the school listing often shows capacity or reviews mentioning class sizes. The school's own website frequently states enrollment capacity. You can also estimate from the number of WhatsApp parent groups mentioned in their social media.

---

### Factor 4: Digital Presence
**Weight: 2 points**

**Why it matters:** A director who has a functioning school website and active social media has already made the mental leap to "technology can help us communicate with parents." They will understand the product category immediately. A school with no website and no social media will spend half your 10-minute call asking what an app is. Digital presence is a proxy for tech-awareness, modernity of operations, and receptiveness to new tools — not just for reaching them, but for the quality of the insight you get.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 2 | Has a functioning website (updated within 2 years) AND active Instagram or Facebook (post within last 3 months) |
| 1 | Has a website OR social media, but not both — or has both but both are clearly outdated |
| 0 | No findable website and no findable social media profile |

**How to find this data:** Google the school name + city. Most schools with a web presence appear in the first page of results. Check the website's footer or "last updated" signals (copyright year, blog post dates, news section). For Instagram, search the school name directly on Instagram or via Google ("escuela infantil [name] instagram"). For Facebook, same approach. This is the most time-consuming scoring factor — see Section 3 for a systematic bulk approach using Google Sheets and importXML formulas to semi-automate domain checking.

---

### Factor 5: Has Email Address in Directory
**Weight: 1 point**

**Why it matters:** Having an email address in the EDUCABASE export means you can send a short, warm pre-call email the day before you call. This is not about getting a reply to the email (most will not reply). It is about priming: a director who received an email with your name yesterday will pick up your call more readily, and the call opens with "I sent you a short email yesterday" rather than a cold introduction. Pre-call emails measurably improve connect rates in Spanish SME outreach by approximately 15–20%.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Has a valid email address in the EDUCABASE/Ministerio directory |
| 0 | No email listed, or only a generic webmaster@domain.com with no school-specific contact |

**How to find this data:** Directly from the EDUCABASE export. The field is typically labeled "Email" or "Correo electrónico." Treat emails like "info@centroX.es" as valid (1 point). Discard emails that are clearly ministerial placeholders or that bounce when you test them.

---

### Factor 6: Independent Operator (Not a Chain or Franchise)
**Weight: 1 point**

**Why it matters:** Chains and franchises (Nemomarlin, Pequeños Einsteins, Kimba, Apetito de Saber, Eurocolegio, etc.) have centralized purchasing decisions. Even if the local director loves your product, they will tell you "I'd have to ask the head office." That validation conversation is worthless for your current phase — you are trying to reach decision-makers who can say "yes" unilaterally. Additionally, chains typically have internal communication tools already in place (or mandated), which distorts the pain signal you are trying to measure.

**Scoring:**
| Score | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1 | Clearly independent: unique school name, no franchise branding, own domain |
| 0 | Part of a recognizable chain, franchise, or large cooperative group (Nemomarlin, Eurocolegio, Aldeas Infantiles, Red de Guarderías municipales, etc.) |

**How to find this data:** School name and website are usually sufficient. Red flags for chains: school names that follow a template (e.g., "Escuela Infantil Nemomarlin [city]"), franchise-branded websites that share a template across multiple cities, or a LinkedIn company page showing 50+ schools under the same parent organization. When in doubt, Google "[school name] franquicia" or check if the same branding appears in multiple Spanish cities. Also check: if the school's website domain is shared with other schools (e.g., nemomarlin.es/centro-X), it is a chain.

**Call status values (Estado):**
- `Sin contactar` — Not yet attempted
- `Intento fallido` — Called, no answer or wrong number
- `Contactado — no director` — Reached someone but not the director
- `Director contactado` — Reached the director, call scheduled or in progress
- `Entrevista completada` — Full 10–15 min discovery conversation done
- `No interesado` — Director declined to speak
- `Descartado` — Removed from list (chain, wrong type, etc.)

**Signal rating scale (post-call, 1–5):**
- 1 — No pain confirmed. Director said "no tenemos ese problema" or equivalent.
- 2 — Mild acknowledgment. "Sí, a veces hay problemas, pero no es grave."
- 3 — Clear problem. Director described a real pain point without prompting.
- 4 — Strong pain. Director gave specific examples and asked about your solution.
- 5 — Extreme pain. Director said some version of "when can I have this?" or offered to introduce you to other directors.

### How to Populate This Table Using Spain's Public School Directory

**Step 1: Access EDUCABASE**

Go to: https://www.educacion.gob.es/centros/buscarCentros.do

This is the Ministerio de Educación y Formación Profesional's official school search portal. It allows filtering by:
- Comunidad Autónoma
- Provincia / Municipio
- Tipo de centro (Público / Privado / Concertado)
- Enseñanzas impartidas (Educación Infantil Primer Ciclo, Segundo Ciclo, etc.)
- Naturaleza del centro

Alternatively, use the statistics portal ESTADÍSTICA DE LAS ENSEÑANZAS NO UNIVERSITARIAS at: https://www.educacionyfp.gob.es/servicios-al-ciudadano/estadisticas/no-universitaria/centros.html

This portal provides downloadable datasets (usually in Excel or CSV format) with center-level data including name, address, phone, email, titularidad, and enseñanzas.

**Step 2: Apply filters and export**

Apply these filters in the search or dataset:
- Enseñanzas: "Educación Infantil" — specifically check for Primer Ciclo (0–3) or Primer y Segundo Ciclo (0–6)
- Titularidad: Privada or Concertada (run as two separate exports and combine)
- Comunidad Autónoma: Run one export per region based on your geographic mix

Download as CSV or Excel. You should get between 800–3,000 records per region for Madrid/Catalunya, fewer for smaller regions.

**Step 3: Clean the data**

In Google Sheets or Excel:
1. Delete public schools (Titularidad = Público) if they slipped through
2. Delete schools with no phone number listed (you cannot call them)
3. Standardize phone number format (some will have spaces, dashes, or country codes)
4. Add a blank column for Website, Instagram, and each scoring factor
5. Sort by Comunidad Autónoma, then by city, to enable geographic batching

**Step 4: Score schools you can score immediately**

From the EDUCABASE data alone, you can immediately score:
- Factor 1 (Titularidad): direct from export field
- Factor 2 (Ciclos): direct from enseñanzas field
- Factor 5 (Email): direct from email field
- Factor 6 (Independent): school name pattern check (takes 30 seconds per school to Google chain names)

Factors 3 (size) and 4 (digital presence) require a second research pass — do these only for schools that scored 4+ on the first pass.

### How to Find Website and Social Media at Scale (Not One-by-One)

Doing this manually for 150 schools one at a time takes 5–7 hours. Use this faster approach:

**Option A: Google Sheets batch formula method**

In Google Sheets, add a formula column next to each school name:

```
=HYPERLINK("https://www.google.com/search?q=" & ENCODEURL(A2 & " " & B2 & " guardería"), "Google")
```

Where A2 is the school name and B2 is the city. This creates a clickable Google search link for each school. You can then open 10 tabs at once, scan results in 20–30 seconds per school, and paste the website URL and Instagram handle directly. With this approach, 150 schools take 90–120 minutes instead of 5–7 hours.

**Option B: Google Sheets + ImportXML (semi-automated)**

For checking whether a domain exists and is active, you can use ImportXML to pull page titles from URLs. This requires knowing the domain first, which you often do not have in advance. Use Option A to find domains, then use ImportXML to verify they are live:

```
=IFERROR(IMPORTXML("https://" & C2, "//title"), "No title found")
```

Where C2 contains the domain you found in Option A. A result of "No title found" or an error means the site is down or does not exist.

**Option C: Bulk Instagram search**

Go to Instagram and search "[school name] [city]" for each school. Most Spanish guarderías that are active on Instagram use their exact school name as their handle. If you find them, note the handle and check: when was the last post? Posts within the last 3 months = active (score 2). Posts 3–12 months ago = borderline (score 1). No Instagram found or inactive = score 0 for social component.

**Option D: Google Maps batch check**

Search the school on Google Maps. A school with a Google Maps listing that has 10+ reviews and a "Website" button linked is almost certainly scoring 2 on the digital factor. A school with no Google Maps listing at all is likely a 0. Google Maps is faster to scan than visiting each website individually.

## 4. The First 50 Schools — Selection Rationale

### Allocation by Autonomous Community

Based on the geographic strategy in Section 1:

| Autonomous Community | Schools in first 50 | Score threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Comunidad de Madrid | 17 | 7+ |
| Catalunya | 10 | 7+ |
| Comunitat Valenciana | 9 | 6+ |
| Andalucía | 7 | 6+ |
| País Vasco | 5 | 7+ |
| Galicia or Castilla y León | 2 | 6+ |

**Why a lower threshold (6+) for Valenciana, Andalucía, and the reserve?**
You want enough geographic diversity that if Madrid schools score unusually well on average (because Madrid has more tech-aware directors), you do not end up with a Madrid-only first 50. The 6+ threshold for the southern and eastern regions ensures geographic breadth without including noise.

**Score threshold for inclusion:** 7+ for Madrid and País Vasco, 6+ for all others. Any school scoring 5 or below goes into a backup list, not the first 50.

**Types to prioritize in first 50:**
- Schools that score 2 on Factor 2 (serves 0–3 age group) — highest pain, most relevant for your core use case
- Schools with an email address (Factor 5 = 1) — allows pre-call email warm-up
- Schools in provincial capitals or large cities within each region (not rural towns)

**Types to exclude from first 50 even if score is 7+:**
- Any school where you found LinkedIn evidence of a chain parent company
- Any school where the phone number is a mobile starting with 6 and the school has no other web presence (often individuals running informal home-based "guarderías" that are not commercially interesting)
- Schools where the EDUCABASE record shows "Enseñanzas: Educación Infantil Segundo Ciclo" only, even if their name includes "0–6" (directory errors are common — verify)

## 5. What NOT to Do

### Do Not Call Public Schools (Centros Públicos)

Public guarderías and escuelas infantiles (titularidad pública) cannot purchase your product without going through a formal procurement process (licitación pública or contratación menor). Even in the best case, the director will say "me parece muy interesante pero tendría que hablarlo con el Ayuntamiento / la Consejería de Educación." That conversation is a 12–24 month procurement cycle, not a SaaS sale. Worse, calling public school directors for validation will give you distorted pain signals — their pain is real, but their ability to act on it unilaterally is zero, which will make their enthusiasm misleading. You will waste validation calls on non-buyers.

How to identify them: "Titularidad: Pública" in EDUCABASE. Also look for school names that include "CEIP" (Centro de Educación Infantil y Primaria), "EI Municipal," or school names followed by a famous person's name (EI Federico García Lorca, CEIP Miguel de Cervantes) — these are almost always public. Guarderías Municipales are always public.

### Do Not Call Chains or Franchise Networks

Major chains to exclude from your list:
- **Nemomarlin** (one of Spain's largest private chains, 30+ centers)
- **Pequesol / Los Peques** (regional chains in eastern Spain)
- **Kimba** (Madrid-area chain)
- **Eurocolegio Casvi** and affiliated networks
- **Apetito de Saber**
- **The Tiny House** and similar premium English-language franchise groups
- **Aldeas Infantiles SOS** (NGO, centralized)
- **Fundación ONCE** affiliated centers
- Any school whose website shares a template with 5+ other schools across Spain

Why: Even if the local director has personal pain and personal interest, they will tell you "tengo que consultarlo con dirección general" or "aquí los sistemas los decide la central." That insight is not useful for your validation phase. You need to talk to decision-makers who can say yes or no on their own. Chain directors also have a systematically different profile — they are more likely to already have internal tools mandated from above, giving you a false negative on the pain question.

How to identify chains at scale: Google the school name. If the first result is a corporate parent website with a "nuestros centros" section listing 10+ schools, it is a chain. Check LinkedIn for the company — a private guardería with 10+ LinkedIn employees is suspicious. Look at the website domain: if the domain is a brand name that appears in multiple cities (nemomarlin.es, kidsco.es, etc.), it is a chain.

### Other Pitfalls Specific to the Spanish Education Market

**Do not call during oposiciones season (March–June):** In this period, many teachers and even directors are moonlighting as exam preparers or are distracted by staffing uncertainties. Connect rates drop and calls feel rushed.

**Do not assume the person who answers is the director:** In Spanish guarderías, the person who answers the phone is often the administrative assistant, a teacher covering the front desk, or the owner's family member. Always ask: "¿Podría hablar con la directora o directora pedagógica del centro?" Do not pitch to the first person who picks up.

**Do not send a long email before calling:** A cold email longer than 5 lines will be ignored. If you send a pre-call email, keep it to 3 sentences maximum: who you are, what you are doing (research, not sales), and one specific question about whether they would take a 10-minute call. Subject line should not contain any word that reads like advertising (offer, gratis, solución, plataforma, aplicación).

**Do not confuse "guardería" with "escuela infantil" in your outreach language:** In Spain, "guardería" is informally used but technically refers only to the 0–3 cycle and implies a more custodial model. Many school directors, especially those who take a pedagogical approach, prefer "escuela infantil" or "centro de educación infantil." Using "guardería" with the wrong director signals you do not understand their sector. Use "escuela infantil" or "centro de educación infantil" as the default.

**Do not call in August:** Even if a school is technically open in summer (some guarderías run verano programs), August in Spain is when everyone is in holiday mode. Directors are either on vacation, covering for staff who are on vacation, or mentally absent. Any data you collect in August will be low quality.

**Do not combine validation with a sales pitch on the same call:** The moment you start describing features or pricing, the director will become defensive and the conversation will shift from honest problem exploration to a sales objection mode. You will lose the insight. If a director asks "and do you have something already built?", the correct answer is: "We are at the research stage — I am not selling anything today. I am trying to understand whether this problem is worth building a solution for, and your perspective would be incredibly valuable."

## 6. Tracking and Iteration

### Recommended Tool: Google Sheets (First Choice), Airtable (Second Choice)

**Google Sheets** is the right tool for a solo founder in validation phase. Reasons:
- Free
- Shareable instantly (you can share the sheet with a co-founder or advisor with one link)
- Formulas for auto-calculating total score work immediately
- Easy to add conditional formatting to highlight Tier 1 rows in green
- Google Sheets on mobile is usable enough to update call notes immediately after a call from your phone

**Airtable** is better if you want a Kanban view of call statuses (which is visually nicer for managing a pipeline) but the free plan limits rows, and the setup time is not justified at this phase.

**Notion** is not recommended for this use case. Notion's table views are slower and less formula-friendly than Sheets. It is better suited for storing the interview transcripts and insights after calls are done.

---

### What to Do When You Get No Answer

Follow this sequence per school before marking as exhausted:

| Attempt | Timing | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Attempt 1 | Day 1, 09:45 | Cold call. No answer — log as "Intento fallido 1." |
| Attempt 2 | Day 3, 10:15 | Call again, different time window. No answer — log "Intento fallido 2." |
| Email (if available) | Day 4 | Send 3-sentence cold email (template in separate doc). Subject: "Investigación sobre comunicación con familias." |
| Attempt 3 | Day 7, 16:15 | Final call attempt. If no answer — mark "Descartado — sin respuesta." |

**Three attempts + one email is the maximum.** Beyond that, the opportunity cost of a fourth attempt exceeds the expected value of the connection. Move on to the next school on the list.

**Exception:** If you reach someone at the school (not the director) and they tell you the director will be available at a specific time, that counts as a scheduled callback, not an "attempt." Log it as "Callback programado" and call at the specified time — this is a warm lead.

---

### When to Send a Cold Email Instead of Calling

Send email first (before calling) in these cases:
- The school has a clear email listed, a professional website, and an active Instagram — these directors are digitally responsive and may prefer email to cold calls
- The school is in a city where you plan to visit in person for a pilot — a softer first touch sets up a better in-person meeting
- It is late April or May and the school year is winding down — email has a longer shelf life than a call that gets forgotten

Always call first (skip email) in these cases:
- No email listed in directory
- Small school in a non-major city where the director likely checks email infrequently
- Schools you want to contact urgently before the Semana Santa break

**Email template (Spanish, 3 sentences max):**

> Asunto: Investigación sobre comunicación con familias en escuelas infantiles
>
> Hola [nombre si lo tienes, otherwise "Buenos días"],
>
> Mi nombre es [nombre], y estoy investigando cómo los centros de educación infantil gestionan la comunicación diaria con las familias. No os vendo nada — estoy en fase de investigación y me gustaría haceros una pregunta de 10 minutos por teléfono esta semana.
>
> ¿Tendría disponibilidad el director/a para una llamada breve el martes o miércoles por la mañana?
>
> Muchas gracias,
> [nombre y teléfono]

### How to Iterate the List When Early Calls Reveal a Better Target Profile

Plan for list iteration after your first 10 completed calls. At that point, stop and do a quick analysis:

**Questions to ask yourself after 10 calls:**

1. Are schools with a score of 8+ converting to conversations at a higher rate than 6–7 schools? If yes, raise the threshold for the remaining 40 schools.
2. Are schools in a specific city generating better signal (higher average signal rating)? If yes, over-weight that city in the next batch.
3. Is Factor 2 (Ciclo age range) the strongest predictor of pain? If schools serving only 0–3 are giving you 4–5 signal ratings while 0–6 schools give you 2–3, shift your list toward more 0–3 guarderías.
4. Are any specific school characteristics not in your scoring system correlating with high-signal calls? Common surprises: family-owned schools (vs. professionally managed), schools that mention WhatsApp explicitly on their website, schools that recently opened (under 5 years, still in growth mode and feeling the pain of scale).
5. Is a specific region underperforming on connect rate? If Madrid schools are answering but Valencia schools are not, reallocate the next 50 toward Madrid.

**Concrete iteration rule:** After every 10 completed calls, do a 30-minute debrief. Adjust the score weighting or add a new factor if the data suggests it. Document the change and the reason in a separate "Research Log" note.
