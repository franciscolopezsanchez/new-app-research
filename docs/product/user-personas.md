# User Personas — School-Parent Communication Platform
**Product Area**: Core Platform
**Last Updated**: 2026-03-20
**Version**: 1.0
**Status**: Draft — awaiting validation through user interviews (target: 5 interviews per persona before roadmap lock)

---

## Purpose of This Document

These personas represent our three core user archetypes for the Spanish preschool market (ages 0–6). They are based on known behavioral patterns of preschool staff and parents in Spain, the dominant current-state tool (WhatsApp + paper notes), and the SaaS school-pays business model.

These are hypotheses. Each persona should be pressure-tested against real interviews before any major roadmap decision.

---

## Persona 1 — The School Director (Buyer / Decision-Maker)

### Profile
- **Name**: Carmen Ruiz
- **Age**: 44
- **Location**: Madrid suburbs (Pozuelo de Alarcón)
- **Role**: Director and co-owner of a private preschool, 80 children enrolled, 8 staff members
- **Education**: Early childhood education degree, some business administration background

### Background
Carmen has run her school for 11 years. She manages operations, handles parent relations, does some teaching coverage, and is responsible for the school's finances. She evaluates and signs off on every operational tool the school uses.

Her current setup is a patchwork: a WhatsApp group per classroom, a paper incident book, email for formal notices, and a private Facebook group nobody checks. She knows it is not sustainable and is embarrassed when parents complain about missed messages.

### Goals and Motivations
- Differentiate the school from municipal (public) preschools on quality and service
- Reduce time spent managing parent communications so teachers can focus on children
- Build trust and transparency with parents — especially new September enrollments
- Demonstrate that each child's day is well-documented and professionally managed
- Keep administrative overhead low enough that the school remains profitable

### Pain Points with Current Tools
- WhatsApp groups create chaos: parents reply-all, unrelated conversations derail important notices, teachers feel obligated to respond at 10pm
- No separation between personal and professional: teachers use personal phone numbers (GDPR exposure)
- Paper-based incident and activity logs are time-consuming and can be lost
- No central record of what was communicated to which parent — cannot audit conversations
- New parents in September expect a modern digital experience; current setup feels unprofessional

### Technology Comfort Level
**Moderate.** Comfortable with smartphones, WhatsApp, Google Drive, and basic spreadsheets. Does not want complex software requiring training sessions. Will evaluate the product on a tablet before buying.

### What Success Looks Like
- Teachers stop using personal WhatsApp for school communications within the first month
- Parents say at pickup "I love the app — I feel so connected to what [child] is doing"
- She can pull up any message, report, or incident from any date in under 30 seconds
- Zero GDPR incidents involving parent photos or child data

### Key Quote
> "I want parents to feel like they chose the best school in the neighborhood — and I want my teachers to go home without their phones buzzing with parent messages. Right now I can't give them either."

## Persona 2 — The Teacher (Primary Daily User)

### Profile
- **Name**: Laura Martínez
- **Age**: 28
- **Location**: Barcelona (Eixample district)
- **Role**: Lead teacher in the 2–3 year-old classroom, 14 children, one teaching assistant
- **Education**: Grado en Educación Infantil

### Background
Laura is energetic, child-centered, and passionate about early development. She takes photos on her personal iPhone during the day, then uploads them to WhatsApp in the evening — doing unpaid work at home and blending her personal and professional digital lives.

She has seen ClassDojo used during training and thought it looked good, but her current school has not adopted anything formal.

### Goals and Motivations
- Give each child's family a genuine window into their child's day
- Complete documentation and communication during school hours, not after
- Feel professionally recognized: a well-presented daily report reflects the quality of her work
- Avoid being the target of anxious parent messages at 9pm
- Reduce repetition: currently writes the same update in 14 separate WhatsApp chats

### Pain Points with Current Tools
- Sending individual photo updates via WhatsApp to 14 families is manual, repetitive, and eats into personal time
- Parents respond at all hours and expect immediate replies — no "off switch"
- Cannot share a photo with the whole class without inadvertently sharing one child's image with all other families (GDPR violation)
- When a parent asks "what did my child eat last Tuesday?", she has to dig through paper records
- Double work: paper incident forms + separate parent WhatsApp message for same event

### Technology Comfort Level
**High.** Laura is a digital native, comfortable with iOS, Android, Instagram, and Google Workspace. She will learn a new tool quickly if it saves her time. Tolerance for bad UX is low — she will quietly stop using a tool that slows her down.

### What Success Looks Like
- She can send a daily activity summary with 3–5 photos to all 14 families in under 3 minutes
- She does not receive a single work-related message after 6pm
- Parents arrive at pickup already knowing the day's highlights
- She has a searchable digital record of every child's activities, meals, and incidents

### Key Quote
> "I spend more time managing parent WhatsApp groups than I do planning activities. That is not why I became a teacher. If there was an app where I just tap a few things and all the parents are updated — I would use it every single day."

## Persona 3 — The Parent (End Consumer / Influence on Renewal)

### Profile
- **Name**: Alejandro and Sofia Torres
- **Ages**: Alejandro 35, Sofia 33
- **Location**: Valencia (Campanar neighborhood)
- **Child**: Mateo, 2.5 years old, first year at preschool
- **Occupation**: Alejandro — logistics manager (hybrid, some travel); Sofia — marketing at a mid-size company

### Background
Mateo is their first child. Leaving him at preschool for the first time was emotionally difficult. Sofia checks her phone frequently during the day hoping for an update. Alejandro, who travels occasionally, feels disconnected from Mateo's school life.

Currently the school sends photos via a classroom WhatsApp group. It is better than nothing, but the group is noisy and important notices get buried in conversation.

### Goals and Motivations
- Know that Mateo is safe, happy, and having a good day — every single day
- Feel actively included in his early development, not just informed after the fact
- Have a lasting record of Mateo's milestones and memories from these early years
- Trust that the school is professional and organized — communication quality signals care quality
- For Alejandro: stay connected even when traveling, without relying on Sofia to relay everything

### Pain Points with Current Tools
- WhatsApp classroom group is noisy; important notices get buried
- Photos in group chat feel informal and impermanent — must manually save each one
- No way to know if a message to the teacher has been read
- Basic information (did he eat? did he sleep?) is not proactively delivered — must ask
- No structured view of Mateo's week or month — everything scattered across chat history

### Technology Comfort Level
**High.** Both parents are smartphone-first with no resistance to new apps if value is immediate and clear. Sofia will become a product advocate if the experience is excellent. Single friction point: app download fatigue — they will not install just because the school asks. Onboarding must deliver value on day one.

### What Success Looks Like
- Sofia opens the app at 11am, sees a photo of Mateo painting with a short note, puts her phone down feeling calm
- Alejandro, on a work trip, gets a push notification with a photo of Mateo at snack time
- At the end of the day they look at the daily report together and know exactly what Mateo ate, did, and how he seemed
- Sofia recommends the school to a colleague and specifically mentions the app

### Key Quote
> "I just want to know he is okay. Not at pickup — during the day. One photo and a line of text telling me he is happy and ate his lunch. That is all I need. Right now I spend half the morning anxious because I have heard nothing."

## Persona Interaction Map

| Interaction | Carmen (Director) | Laura (Teacher) | Alejandro/Sofia (Parent) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Platform decision | Decides to buy | Influences adoption | Influences renewal and churn |
| Daily active use | Low (oversight) | High (content creation) | Medium (consumption) |
| Value realized | Operational efficiency, compliance, parent retention | Time saved, professional recognition | Peace of mind, connection, memories |
| Risk of churning the platform | If admin burden is high or ROI unclear | If UX is slow or adds work | If updates are sparse or feel impersonal |
| Onboarding priority | Must see admin dashboard value in first session | Must complete first daily report in under 5 minutes | Must receive first update before first week ends |

## Open Discovery Questions

Answer these through user interviews before the V2 roadmap is locked.

- [ ] How many preschools currently use a formal digital tool (non-WhatsApp) for parent communication, and what drove the switch?
- [ ] What is the average number of messages a teacher sends per day per family?
- [ ] What percentage of parents check their phones during working hours specifically for school updates?
- [ ] Is a "family timeline / memory book" feature a genuine differentiator or a nice-to-have that drives low engagement?
- [ ] Do parents want two-way messaging or is read-only updates sufficient for daily use?
- [ ] How do schools currently handle GDPR consent for child photos, and how aware are directors of non-compliance?
- [ ] What is the maximum monthly per-child price Carmen would pay before the conversation shifts to "too expensive"?
